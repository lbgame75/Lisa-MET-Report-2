**Example 1**  
**Cognitive Abilities:**  
Wyatt was administered the Comprehensive Test of Nonverbal Intelligence, Second Edition (CTONI-2) to evaluate his nonverbal reasoning and problem-solving abilities using tasks that minimize language demands. The CTONI-2 requires students to demonstrate understanding by pointing to responses rather than providing verbal explanation, which made it well-suited for Wyatt given his communication profile. Wyatt participated across two sessions. During the first session, he frequently needed the examiner to physically point to where he should be looking on the page before he could orient to the task. By the second session, he was doing this more independently, though his accuracy across both sessions was similar. His scores are considered a valid estimate of his current nonverbal reasoning abilities.

Wyatt's overall Full Scale score of 71 falls in the Below Average range (3rd percentile). His scores were similar whether tasks used real-life images or abstract geometric shapes, which means his reasoning difficulties are not specific to one type of visual material. They are broad.

The CTONI-2 measures three types of thinking across both pictorial and geometric formats. Analogies tasks ask the student to figure out how two things are related and then apply that same relationship to a new pair. Categories tasks ask the student to identify what several items have in common and find what belongs with them. Sequences tasks ask the student to recognize a visual pattern and determine what comes next.

Wyatt's weakest scores were on Analogies and Sequences subtests, both at the 5th percentile. Analogies were hard for him because they require figuring out how two things are related and then applying that same relationship somewhere new, which is a type of thinking that cannot be solved just by recognizing something familiar. Sequences were similarly difficult. Even when a visual pattern was present, figuring out the rule and extending it was not reliable for him. Categories were somewhat stronger, at the 16th percentile. When the task was to find what things had in common from a set, he had more success, likely because that kind of grouping relies more on recognition than on reasoning through a novel rule.

This pattern matters for how Wyatt learns. He does better when he can recognize something familiar than when he has to work out something new on his own. New tasks, new settings, and new routines will require direct teaching, clear modeling, and repeated practice before he can apply them independently. He is unlikely to generalize a skill from one setting to another without that explicit support. For transition planning, this means that vocational tasks and daily living routines will need to be taught directly in the environments where he will use them, not assumed to transfer once learned in the classroom.

**Example 2**  
**Cognitive Abilities:**  
Quenton was administered the Comprehensive Test of Nonverbal Intelligence, Second Edition (CTONI-2) to evaluate his nonverbal reasoning and problem-solving abilities using tasks that minimize language demands.  The CTONI-2 requires students to demonstrate understanding by pointing to responses rather than providing verbal explanation.  Quenton was extremely cooperative throughout the assessment.  He followed directions carefully, demonstrated full effort on all tasks, and correctly answered all sample items, indicating clear understanding of expectations. He worked thoughtfully rather than impulsively, consistently scanned all five response options before selecting an answer and occasionally reconsidered and changed responses prior to moving on.  Attention and persistence were strong. Results are considered a valid representation of his current nonverbal reasoning abilities.

**CTONI-2**

| SUBTESTS | Scaled Score | Percentile Rank | Classification |
| :---- | :---: | :---: | :---: |
| Pictorial Analogies | 5 | 5th | Below Average |
| Geometric Analogies | 5 | 5th | Below Average |
| Pictorial Categories | 8 | 25th | Average |
| Geometric Categories | 7 | 16th | Low Average |
| Pictorial Sequences | 7 | 16th | Low Average |
| Geometric Sequences | 7 | 16th | Low Average |
| COMPOSITE | Standard Score | Percentile Rank | Classification |
| Pictorial Scale | 77 | 6th | Below Average |
| Geometric Scale | 76 | 5th | Below Average |
| Full Scale | 74 | 4th | Below Average |

Quenton’s overall Full-Scale Nonverbal IQ of 74 falls within the Below Average range. His scores were similar across pictorial tasks that used real life images and geometric tasks that used abstract shapes, suggesting that his reasoning skills are consistent whether the material is concrete or abstract.  

His lowest scores were on Analogies tasks (5th percentile), which required him to figure out how two items were related and apply that same relationship to a new pair.  These tasks were difficult for him when the pattern was not obvious.  He performed better on Categories and Sequences tasks (16th-25th percentile), showing more success when identifying similarities or following a clear visual progression.  

In classroom and post-secondary settings, Quenton will perform best when tasks are concrete, clearly modeled, and structured with clear visual examples.  He may have difficulty independently inferring rules or applying learning to new situations without direct instruction.  As he prepares for adult transition, he will benefit from environments that provide predictable routines, step-by-step modeling, and repeated practice. Consistent structure and clear expectations will support continued growth and independence.
