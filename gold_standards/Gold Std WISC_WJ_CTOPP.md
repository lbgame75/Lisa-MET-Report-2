**Cognitive:**  
Violet was administered the WISC-V to evaluate her cognitive functioning, including verbal reasoning, nonverbal reasoning, working memory, and processing speed, and to understand how these skills relate to her classroom learning. She was also administered selected subtests from the Woodcock-Johnson IV Tests of Cognitive Abilities to further examine long-term memory and retrieval. Violet was extremely cooperative and socially engaged throughout testing. Once rapport was established, she was motivated to do well and frequently sought reassurance that she was performing adequately. She demonstrated nervous laughter at times and was visibly sensitive to challenge, occasionally stating, “I feel so dumb,” or “I don’t know how to start.” When tasks became difficult, she showed frustration and briefly shut down verbally, though with encouragement she persisted. She was easily distracted by hallway noises and visually busy pages, particularly those with small font or dense visual arrays. Despite this, she maintained effort and completed all tasks. Results are considered a valid estimate of her current cognitive functioning.

Violet’s overall cognitive ability fell in the Below Average range (Full Scale IQ \= 81; 10th percentile). She showed stronger performance on working memory and processing speed tasks, and significantly weaker performance in fluid reasoning and long-term retrieval.

Her Verbal Comprehension Index (SS \= 78; 7th percentile) fell in the Below Average range. On Similarities and Vocabulary, she was hesitant to respond and frequently prefaced answers with “I don’t know,” even when she had partial understanding. She often needed items to be repeated, and queries from the examiner to “tell me more”.  She appeared reluctant to risk being wrong and required encouragement to attempt responses. This pattern suggests that while she has access to everyday language, abstract verbal reasoning and explaining conceptual relationships is very difficult and effortful. In classroom settings, this may show up when she needs to explain her thinking, define terms precisely, or discuss abstract ideas.

Violet’s Visual Spatial Index (SS \= 84; 14th percentile) also fell in the Below Average range. On Block Design, she worked quickly and accurately on simpler designs but became confused when designs rotated into a diamond orientation. She sometimes constructed correct designs that were rotated incorrectly and had difficulty shifting her perspective. When visual demands increased, she sighed, became visibly overwhelmed, and stated she did not know how to begin. This indicates that organizing and mentally manipulating visual information, especially when it requires flexibility, is challenging. In the classroom, this may affect tasks such as interpreting diagrams, organizing written work on a page, or understanding math problems that require visual restructuring.

Fluid Reasoning was her most significant area of weakness (SS \= 74; 4th percentile). On Figure Weights and the follow-up Picture Concepts subtest, her performance fell in the Well Below Average range. She struggled to identify underlying patterns, shared relationships, or conceptual rules linking items together. Even when she carefully examined all response options, she had difficulty determining what the question was truly asking. She often responded with “absolutely no” or shut down verbally when tasks felt complex, but still looked at items and tried to figure them out. This pattern indicates significant difficulty reasoning through new information and solving problems. In academic settings, this impacts problem solving in mathematics, interpreting word problems, drawing inferences in reading, and adapting to unfamiliar or multi-step tasks.

In contrast, her Working Memory Index (SS \= 91; 27th percentile) fell in the Average range. On Digit Span, she demonstrated solid ability to hold and repeat information. This suggests that her difficulty is not primarily due to limited short-term memory capacity, but to difficulty organizing and reasoning with information.

Violet’s Processing Speed Index (SS \= 95; 37th percentile) also fell in the Average range. On Coding, she worked efficiently and carefully, even asking permission to erase an error to ensure accuracy. On Symbol Search, she understood the task quickly and sustained her focus for the duration of the task. These scores indicate adequate efficiency on structured, rule-based tasks that are clear and repetitive. However, speed on simple visual tasks does not offset difficulty with complex reasoning demands.

Additional assessment of long-term storage and retrieval through the WJ-IV revealed significant weakness (Long-Term Storage & Retrieval SS \= 72; 3rd percentile). On Story Memory, Violet appeared to focus on memorizing isolated words rather than grasping the overall meaning of the narrative, which limited recall. On Visual Auditory Learning, she struggled to form meaningful associations between symbols and words and did not spontaneously generate connections that would support memory. These results indicate difficulty forming efficient mental links and organizing new information in a way that supports later retrieval. In classroom settings, this may affect her ability to learn new vocabulary, retain multi-step concepts over time, or integrate information across lessons.

Violet’s cognitive profile helps explain her classroom experience. She can stay engaged and complete structured, clearly defined tasks, when expectations are explicit and the path forward is clear. However, when she is asked to figure out how to begin, identify patterns, draw connections, or solve unfamiliar problems, she becomes overwhelmed and unsure of her thinking. Tasks such as multi-step math problems, abstract reasoning in literature or history, organizing longer written responses, and independently breaking down projects are likely to feel confusing and difficult. She benefits from direct modeling of how to approach a task, explicit teaching of the “why” behind patterns or concepts, guided practice before working independently, and opportunities to ask questions in a low-pressure setting. When thinking steps are made visible and assignments are broken into manageable parts, she is more likely to stay engaged and persist rather than shut down.
