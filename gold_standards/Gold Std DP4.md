Developmental Profile, Fourth Edition (DP-4)  
Because individual cognitive testing yielded limited valid data due to significant difficulty sustaining attention and responding to structured tasks, the DP-4 was completed by Suma’s father and his classroom teacher to document current developmental functioning and inform present levels for IEP planning.

The DP-4 measures development across five areas: Physical, Adaptive Behavior, Social-Emotional, Cognitive, and Communication.  Suma’s scores across domains fell in the Extremely Low range, indicating developmental skills significantly below age expectations across both home and school.

|  Physical The Physical scale measures large muscle coordination and fine motor control used for movement and classroom tasks. Parent ratings place Suma in the early preschool range, while teacher ratings place him in the late toddler range. Across settings, motor functioning falls between approximately 2 and 4 years of age and is significantly below fifth-grade expectations. Across home and school, Suma walks independently, climbs stairs with support, carries objects safely, and demonstrates basic hand control such as twisting motions and stacking objects. These skills show stable gross motor mobility in both environments. However, both raters report that he does not consistently catch a ball, balance on one foot, jump over obstacles, cut with scissors independently, draw shapes such as a square, or tie shoelaces. Fine motor precision and coordinated motor planning remain significantly delayed. Motor abilities support independent movement but limit participation in structured fine motor tasks required in the classroom. Adaptive Behavior The Adaptive Behavior scale measures independence in daily routines, self-care, safety, and task completion. Both parent and teacher ratings place Suma’s adaptive functioning in the toddler range. In both environments, Suma can drink from a cup, use utensils, remove some clothing items, assist with dressing, and use basic digital devices. He demonstrates awareness of hunger or thirst and can follow simple routines when prompted. These skills show participation in daily routines with support. However, across settings he does not dress independently, does not manage toileting independently for bowel movements, does not clean up after activities, does not use coping strategies when upset, and does not complete tasks without frequent adult reminders. At school, he does not complete assignments independently or manage materials. At home, he does not independently manage safety information or personal care needs. Adaptive functioning indicates consistent dependence on direct adult prompting and supervision across settings. Social-Emotional The Social-Emotional scale measures interaction, engagement, and awareness of social expectations. Parent ratings place Suma’s social functioning in the infant range, while teacher ratings place him in the very early toddler range. Across both settings, social development remains significantly below age expectations. Across settings, Suma responds to familiar adults, shows enjoyment during preferred activities, reacts differently to familiar versus unfamiliar people, and can be calmed through adult contact. These behaviors demonstrate basic emotional responsiveness and attachment. However, both raters indicate that he does not initiate peer interaction, does not sustain play with other children, does not engage independently in structured tasks for extended periods, does not demonstrate awareness of others’ feelings, and does not adjust behavior based on social setting. He needs direct adult support to interact with peers and participate in group activities. Cognitive The Cognitive scale measures early problem solving, concept understanding, and foundational preacademic skills. Both parent and teacher ratings place Suma’s cognitive development within the early toddler range. Across home and school, Suma follows moving objects visually, responds to sounds, searches for hidden objects, identifies body parts, and demonstrates early counting skills. Teacher ratings indicate he can count to 15 verbally and recognize his name in print. These skills show emerging awareness of symbols and quantity. However, in both settings he does not sort by color or shape consistently, does not understand size or position concepts, does not grasp early number concepts beyond rote counting, does not write recognizable letters or numbers, does not read functional words, and does not perform simple mental math. Concept development remains concrete and requires direct modeling and repetition. Communication The Communication scale measures receptive and expressive language abilities. Parent and teacher ratings place Suma’s communication skills in the early toddler range. Across settings, Suma uses facial expressions appropriately, imitates gestures and some spoken words, follows simple one-step directions and some two-step directions, and uses gestures or limited words to request needs. He can imitate short phrases and express basic wants. However, he does not consistently combine words into phrases, does not answer questions about stories, does not follow multi-step directions, does not retell events, does not engage in sustained back-and-forth conversation, and does not adjust communication to social context. Communication supports basic needs but does not support independent academic engagement or reciprocal peer interaction. |  |  |  |  |  |  |

Overall, DP-4 results from both home and school place Suma’s developmental functioning between infancy and early preschool levels across domains. Baseline data indicate global developmental delays requiring intensive adult support, structured instruction and individualized programming to access and participate meaningfully in educational activities.
