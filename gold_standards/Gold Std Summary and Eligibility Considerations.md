**SUMMARY AND RECOMMENDATIONS**  
Kalem is a seventh grade student who was reevaluated for special education to better understand his emotional and behavioral functioning, cognitive skills, academic learning, and communication needs so the team can determine the supports that will help him participate successfully at school.

Results of the current evaluation indicate that Kalem demonstrates Below Average overall cognitive functioning (WISC-V FSIQ \= 76, 5th percentile) with a significant working memory weakness (Digit Span \= 1st percentile), along with academic achievement skills that fall well below grade-level expectations across reading, writing, and mathematics (WIAT-IV scores primarily in the 2nd–12th percentile range). His cognitive profile reflects uneven development rather than a global cognitive impairment. Kalem approached testing with a positive attitude, remained cooperative and regulated during challenging tasks, and demonstrated strong effort and compliance throughout the evaluation sessions.

Academically, Kalem has significant foundational skill gaps that interfere with his ability to independently access grade-level instruction. In reading, he is able to derive general meaning from text but struggles with word-level decoding, reading fluency, and answering inferential questions. In mathematics, limited number sense and lack of automatic basic skills place a high cognitive load on problem solving, resulting in slow pace and drained mental energy. In written expression, Kalem demonstrates idea generation and willingness to engage in writing tasks; however, weaknesses in working memory, organization, and grammar/mechanics impact his ability to sustain focus, follow task requirements, and produce coherent written output. Across academic areas, his performance reflects difficulty managing multiple demands simultaneously.

Socially and behaviorally, Kalem currently presents as polite, cooperative, and with improved emotional regulation compared to earlier school years. Teacher input and classroom observation indicate that he is most successful when expectations are clear, cognitive demands are reduced, and he is able to complete work in a quieter, more structured setting. Kalem continues to rely on adult support to manage transitions, regulate stamina, and navigate the sensory and cognitive demands of general education classrooms. His pattern of leaving class early to complete work in the EI classroom appears to be a coping strategy to manage overload.  Kalem has also shown positive growth in his interactions with peers and adults.

Eligibility Considerations  
In terms of special education, Kalem exhibits criteria consistent with multiple areas of eligibility: OHI, EI, and SLI. Although improvements have been made, Kalem’s emotional and behavioral needs remain relevant. An additional area of eligibility to consider is the category of Other Health Impairment, which can also capture the cognitive and academic deficits identified in addition to the emotional/behavioral needs.  Kalem’s medical conditions: Genetic Disorder 15q13.3 microdeletion, ADHD, Anxiety, Asthma, Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus present significant weaknesses in attention, stamina, memory, and emotional regulation, and executive functioning. Eligibility under the category of Other Health Impairment could be considered.

*Emotional Impairment (EI) \-* Eligibility under the Emotional Impairment category is considered to be a manifestation of behavioral problems primarily in the affective domain, over an extended period of time. These problems result in behaviors manifested by 1 or more of the following characteristics: inability to build or maintain satisfactory interpersonal relationships within the school environment; inappropriate types of behavior or feelings under normal circumstances; general, pervasive mood of unhappiness or depression; or tendency to develop physical symptoms or fears associated with personal or school problems. These behavioral problems interfere with access to and progress in general education to the degree that the student requires special education programs/services. Kalem has a long school history of emotional and behavioral challenges, having been in the EI program in elementary, as well as now in middle school. Though Kalem’s behavioral challenges have improved greatly since starting the EI program in middle school, there are times when he still experiences issues. Kalem has a difficult time transitioning between classes, as well as between the EI room and the general education classroom. Once in class, Kalem does well, but often leaves early to return to the EI classroom for a break, or to work in a quiet spot with help from adults. Kalem often presents with somatic complaints or of being tired and wanting to sleep, though this has improved somewhat this school year.  Kalem has only had one significant behavioral episode where multiple adults were attempting to intervene. Kalem’s genetic condition also plays a role in considering his mental health. When considering the eligibility of Emotional Impairment, based on the evaluation, Kalem continues to meet the criteria for eligibility in this area. 

*Speech-Language Impairment (SLI)*\- Based on the evaluation, Kalem presents with below-average expressive language abilities. His core language was also considered below-average compared to same-aged peers. Kalem’s receptive language is a strength and within the average range. Kalem may have more difficulty expressing himself and what he knows than processing and understanding language. Observations during the evaluation indicate that Kalem’s expressive language can be impacted by stamina and focus. At this time, Kalem’s language profile does meet the criteria for a student with a speech-language impairment based upon the recommendations for speech and language testing and certification by the Michigan Speech-Language-Hearing Association (P.L-27, 2006). The final eligibility determination will be made at the MET meeting.

**Instructional Recommendations:**

* Kalem learns best when directions are short, clear, and given one step at a time, with a quick check to make sure he understands before he starts working.  
    
* Kalem works carefully but slowly. Extra time for reading, writing, and math tasks is recommended so that his effort can result in completed work rather than frustration.  
    
* When reading grade-level material, Kalem would benefit from having the text read aloud or using text-to-speech, which allows him to focus on understanding instead of struggling to decode every word.  
    
* For writing tasks, Kalem needs clear structure every time. Providing graphic organizers, sentence starters, and examples of what the finished work should look like is recommended to help him stay on topic and organize his ideas.  
    
* Allowing Kalem to type instead of handwriting, with spellcheck available, is recommended to reduce errors and help him communicate his ideas more clearly.  
    
* In math, Kalem needs strong visual support and guided practice. Using models, manipulatives, worked examples, and calculators for computation once concepts are taught is recommended so that basic calculation does not prevent him from solving problems.  
    
* Kalem benefits from regular teacher check-ins, especially at the start of work time, to confirm he understands what to do and to help him get started.  
    
* Kalem does better when working in a quieter space or smaller setting, particularly for independent work. Access to a reduced-noise environment is recommended when possible.  
    
* Kalem works hardest when he feels supported. Maintaining calm, patient interactions and consistent adult relationships is recommended to help him stay engaged and regulated.
