Review of Previous Evaluations  
Records that are available for review suggest that Kade was evaluated for special education in Virginia during the 2018-2019 school year when he was in first grade. Early assessment data documented significant concerns in social, emotional, and behavioral functioning that interfered with his ability to participate in a general education classroom. Cognitive testing at that time included the DAS II and the RIAS 2, both of which placed his overall thinking and reasoning skills in the Average range. Despite these age appropriate cognitive abilities, teachers consistently reported clinically significant concerns with hyperactivity, aggression, anxiety, depression, withdrawal, and attention. An Autism Spectrum Rating Scale completed as part of the evaluation produced a profile interpreted as consistent with characteristics of Autism Spectrum Disorder.

Adaptive behavior ratings during this period showed extremely low functioning across conceptual, social, and practical domains, suggesting that although Kade could reason well and learn new information, he struggled with daily independence, social boundaries, communication during frustration, and routines needed for classroom success. Academic testing on the Woodcock Johnson IV and WIAT III reflected mostly Average early reading, writing, and math skills, though teachers noted that emotional reactivity and behavioral outbursts often prevented him from showing these skills consistently. The Virginia IEP documented behavioral episodes including noncompliance, throwing items, falling to the floor, eloping from the classroom, and difficulties maintaining personal space. These needs led the Virginia team to identify him eligible for special education under the category Autism Spectrum Disorder, and he was placed in a highly structured setting with predictable routines and daily behavioral support.

After relocating to Michigan, Kade received an initial evaluation in fourth grade during the 2021 to 2022 school year. Michigan refers to this as an initial evaluation when a student transfers from out of state, even when prior eligibility already exists. The emotional and behavioral concerns noted in Virginia remained present, including difficulty regulating frustration, task avoidance when overwhelmed, and behaviors that disrupted instruction. Speech and language testing showed expressive and receptive language skills in the Low Average range with ongoing difficulty reading social cues, using language to solve problems, and asking for help calmly. Occupational therapy findings reflected fine motor fatigue, handwriting challenges, sensory regulation needs, and functional behaviors that continued to require adult support. The MET did not find evidence of two areas of marked impairments in reciprocal social interactions (only failure to develop peer relationships appropriate to developmental level), and found no qualitative impairments in communication consistent with Autism. His eligibility was changed to Emotional Impairment due to “manifestations of behavioral problems primarily in the affective domain” characterized by the inability to build or maintain satisfactory interpersonal relationships within the school environment, and inappropriate types of behavior or feelings under normal circumstances.

Kade has continued to receive social work services, occupational therapy, and support through the Emotional Impairment classroom. His progress reports show a student who benefits from structured routines, point systems, and direct instruction in emotional regulation. Recent IEP progress data reflects meaningful gains in identifying feelings, naming reasons for dysregulation, and using coping strategies. Staff note that he often recovers more quickly than in prior years and can finish his day positively when supported. At the same time, progress notes also describe periods of dysregulation significant enough to require early dismissal from school, ongoing difficulty minding his own business, and heightened emotional responses to perceived peer misbehavior. These patterns reinforce the continued need for coaching, supervision, and explicit instruction in emotional regulation, social problem solving, and self management.

Across these evaluations, Kade shows a stable pattern of Average cognitive ability paired with significant emotional, behavioral, adaptive, and social communication needs. His history reflects persistent challenges consistent with both Emotional Impairment and Autism Spectrum Disorder.
