**MET Meeting Talking Points** 

Carlo Decina | Grade 9 | PCEP | April 7, 2026 

**Why We Are Here** 

Carlo's father contacted the school counselor in February 2026 with concerns about academic progress and requested a special education evaluation. The team conducted a full initial evaluation to determine whether Carlo meets eligibility criteria for special education under Specific Learning Disability. 

**What the Evaluation Found** 

**Cognitive Functioning (WISC-V)** 

Carlo's overall cognitive functioning falls in the Low Average range (FSIQ 86, 18th percentile). This means he scored higher than 18 out of 100 students his age. Low Average is not a deficit — it describes the lower end of the typical range. 

Relative strengths: 

• Processing Speed (45th percentile) — Average. He works at a normal pace. 

• Visual Spatial Reasoning (55th percentile) — Average. He handles visual and spatial tasks well. Areas in the Low Average range: 

• Verbal Comprehension (18th percentile) — Language-heavy material requires more effort. 

• Fluid Reasoning (12th percentile) — Figuring out new or unfamiliar problems independently is harder for him. 

• Working Memory (21st percentile) — Holding multiple pieces of information in mind at once is more demanding for him than for most peers. 

No cognitive score falls in the deficient range. This is not a profile that indicates a learning disability. 

**Academic Achievement (WIAT-4)** 

Academic scores are consistent with his cognitive profile — Low Average to Average across reading, math, and writing. His highest score was Reading Comprehension (27th percentile, Average range). His lowest was Numerical Operations (10th percentile, Low Average range). No academic score falls below the 10th percentile. 

When cognitive scores and academic scores are this closely matched, a Specific Learning Disability determination is not supported. SLD requires a meaningful gap between what a student should be able to do and what they are actually producing — and that gap is not present here. 

**Historical Data (NWEA)** 

Carlo performed in the Average range in both reading and math from 2nd grade through most of 8th grade. His scores declined at the end of 8th grade and into the first semester of 9th grade. Second semester grades have improved across all courses. This pattern is consistent with a student who struggled with the transition to high school demands, not with a student who has a long-standing disability.  
**What Teachers Are Seeing** 

Teachers across Physics, Algebra I, and English 9 reported a consistent pattern. Carlo is cooperative and courteous, and can engage and think through problems when supported one-on-one. When working independently, he is frequently off-task, rushes through work, and does not follow up when he misses directions or does not understand material. Teachers described him as capable of more than his independent work currently shows. 

**The Eligibility Decision** 

The evaluation does not support eligibility for special education under Specific Learning Disability. The criteria require a meaningful discrepancy between cognitive ability and academic achievement, linked to a processing deficit. Carlo's scores do not show that pattern. His academic performance is consistent with his cognitive profile. 

This does not mean Carlo has no needs. It means those needs do not rise to the level of a disability requiring special education. 

**What Carlo Needs Going Forward** 

The following supports are appropriate and should continue through his RTI plan: • Preferential seating 

• Extended time on tests and quizzes (50%) 

• Alternate testing location 

• Pre-teaching key vocabulary before new units in content-area courses 

• Worked examples and structured problem-solving steps in math 

• Check-ins during independent work time to confirm he knows what to do and redirect before rushing • Explicit support for help-seeking — he does not naturally ask questions when confused 

Counseling support to address engagement and self-advocacy strategies is also worth exploring given the consistent pattern across settings. 

**Questions the Family May Ask** 

**"If he struggles, why doesn't he qualify?"** 

Qualifying for special education requires more than academic difficulty. It requires evidence of a specific disability that is causing the difficulty. Carlo's cognitive and academic scores are consistent with each other — his achievement matches what his cognitive profile predicts. There is no processing deficit driving a gap. His struggles are real, but they are not the result of a learning disability. 

**"What about the Sylvan testing that showed 5th grade reading?"** 

Sylvan's informal assessment is not a standardized measure and cannot be compared directly to nationally normed tests. On the WIAT-4, Carlo's Reading Comprehension fell in the Average range for his age. The two measures are not equivalent. 

**"What happens if things get harder next year?"**  
That is a legitimate concern. His cognitive profile — particularly fluid reasoning and working memory in the Low Average range — will be challenged as course demands increase. The RTI supports need to stay in place and be monitored. If his performance declines significantly again, the team can reconvene and consider whether re-referral is appropriate at that time.
