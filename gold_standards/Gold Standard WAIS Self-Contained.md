**Cognitive Abilities**

Julia was administered the Wechsler Adult Intelligence Scale, Fifth Edition (WAIS-5) to evaluate her cognitive functioning. The WAIS-5 was selected because Julia is verbal, was able to answer questions during the student interview, and presented as a typical-functioning 17-year-old with age-appropriate mannerisms, grooming, and eye contact. It became apparent during the first subtest that there were significant cognitive limitations. Julia was also administered the Comprehensive Test of Nonverbal Intelligence, Second Edition (CTONI-2) as a follow-up measure because the WAIS-5 was completed quickly and Julia was willing to continue working.

Throughout the session, Julia was cooperative, pleasant, and put forth consistent effort. She showed no visible frustration, even when tasks were clearly difficult. She kept working at problems, gave herself time to think before responding, and waited for directions to be given completely before starting. She did not require cueing or redirection at any point. Her attention and stamina were strong throughout the entire session, and her effort, persistence, and willingness to stay with challenging material were notable. Results are considered a valid estimate of her current cognitive abilities.

Julia's Full Scale IQ of 63 fell in the Well Below Average range at the 1st percentile, placing her general cognitive ability approximately two and a half standard deviations below the mean. Nearly all of her subtest scores fell at or below the 2nd percentile. Three scores were relatively stronger within this profile: Vocabulary, Block Design, and Digits Backward, all at the 9th percentile. Vocabulary draws on word knowledge that Julia has built over time through repeated exposure. Block Design is a construction task where Julia could see the model, compare her work to it in real time, and use trial and error with immediate visual feedback. Digits Backward, which was unexpectedly stronger than both Digits Forward and Digit Sequencing, may have engaged a visual-spatial strategy, as some individuals solve this task by visualizing the numbers and reading them in reverse rather than holding them in auditory memory. Julia's visual-spatial processing has been a relative strength across evaluations. The tasks where Julia struggled most were those that required her to solve unfamiliar problems, identify abstract relationships, or extend a pattern without concrete or visual support.

In terms of her Verbal Comprehension abilities, on the Similarities subtest, Julia was asked to identify the relationship between two words. She could handle concrete items, identifying that an apple and a banana are both fruits. When items required abstract reasoning, she answered with a shared function or defined the individual words rather than finding the specific category that linked them. She said "music" for piano and trumpet, rather than identifying them as instruments. She could not identify how a nose and a tongue are alike, or how sticky and fuzzy are alike. On Vocabulary, she performed considerably better. She defined words such as glove, curious, and mend, though she could not define imitate, confide, or transcribe. Julia can acquire and retain vocabulary through repeated exposure, but using language to reason and categorize at an abstract level is significantly harder for her.

In the area of Fluid Reasoning, when answering the training items for Matrix Reasoning, Julia recognized that the task involved patterns and called out "it's a pattern." However, her approach was to find a response that looked like some of the pictures in the problem rather than identifying the rule connecting them. When the relationship required more than visual matching, she could not determine the correct response. Figure Weights was similarly difficult.

Julia's overall Working Memory performance was unique. On Digit Sequencing she was asked to listen to a series of numbers and repeat them back in order from smallest to largest. She struggled to understand the reorganization requirement and typically repeated the numbers back in the order they were given rather than sequencing them. To better understand her memory capacity, the examiner administered two supplemental subtests. On Digits Forward, which simply required repeating numbers as they were heard, Julia also scored in the Well Below Average range suggesting limited auditory memory span. On Digits Backward, which required repeating numbers in reverse order, Julia performed in the Below Average range (scaled score 6, 9th percentile) and was able to repeat a sequence of four numbers backward on one occasion. This was an unexpected finding given the other two memory scores and as suggested above, may be because the backward task engaged a different processing strategy for her. Despite that unique strength, her overall memory pattern indicates that holding and manipulating auditory information is limited, which will affect her ability to follow multi-step directions, retain new verbal information in the moment, and hold multiple pieces of a task in mind while working through it.

The CTONI-2 was administered as a supplemental measure of nonverbal reasoning and problem-solving using tasks that minimize language demands. The CTONI-2 requires students to demonstrate understanding by pointing to responses rather than providing verbal explanation. Julia was cooperative and engaged throughout this measure, consistent with her performance on the WAIS-5. She completed all tasks and her scores are considered a valid estimate of her current nonverbal reasoning abilities.

Julia's Full Scale Nonverbal IQ of 61 falls in the Well Below Average range (below the 1st percentile). Her Pictorial Scale (67, 1st percentile) was somewhat higher than her Geometric Scale (61, below the 1st percentile), indicating that she performed slightly better when tasks used real-life images than when they used abstract geometric shapes. Both scores remain in the Well Below Average range, and her reasoning difficulties are broad.

The CTONI-2 measures three types of thinking across both pictorial and geometric formats. Analogies tasks ask the student to figure out how two things are related and then apply that same relationship to a new pair. Categories tasks ask the student to identify what several items have in common and find what belongs with them. Sequences tasks ask the student to recognize a visual pattern and determine what comes next.

Julia's strongest performance was on Pictorial Categories (scaled score 9, 37th percentile, Average range), which was the highest score across both the WAIS-5 and CTONI-2. On this task, she looked at a set of pictures and selected which one from five options belonged with the group. This is essentially a visual version of the kind of categorical thinking measured by Similarities, but it does not require abstract verbal reasoning or a spoken explanation. Julia was able to determine what the pictures had in common and identify the correct match. On the initial items, she also spontaneously verbalized the category label without being asked to do so, which suggests that her categorical thinking is stronger when she can see the items and does not have to work through the relationship verbally. Geometric Categories was lower (scaled score 6, 9th percentile), indicating that when the same type of task used abstract shapes rather than familiar images, it was harder. Analogies and Sequences tasks were uniformly difficult (all scaled scores of 3, 1st percentile). On Analogies, Julia tended to pick a response that matched some of the pictures in the problem rather than identifying the relationship between items. On Sequences, she could follow simple repeating patterns but did not understand progressions, such as recognizing the stages of a sequence of events shown across pictures.

This pattern is important for how Julia learns and for transition planning. Julia's categorical thinking, her ability to recognize what things have in common and group them together, is a functional strength that can be used in instruction and in vocational settings. Sorting, organizing by category, and recognizing familiar groupings are tasks she can do well. Figuring out new rules, applying a relationship from one situation to another, and understanding progressions or sequences of events are significantly harder. New tasks, new routines, and new environments will require direct teaching, clear modeling, and repeated practice. Julia is unlikely to generalize a skill learned in one setting to a different setting without that explicit support. Vocational tasks and daily living routines will need to be taught directly in the environments where she will use them.
