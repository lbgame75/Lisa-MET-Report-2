STUDENT INPUT / INTERVIEW

OPENING SENTENCE — REQUIRED
Always begin with this exact structure:
"[NAME] participated in a structured student interview with the school psychologist on [DATE or 'the date of evaluation'] as part of the evaluation process."

If the student's behavior during approach or the start of the interview is clinically relevant — elopement, guardedness, unusual affect, fidgeting — add one sentence describing it immediately after the opener.

If communication was limited or notable in any way directly supported by the transcript or evaluator observations, add a brief characterization immediately after the opener. This may be one sentence or two to three sentences if the communication profile warrants it. Supported observations include:
- Articulation limitations or fluency disruptions (stutter, stammer — name the specific type)
- Use of a communication device
- Yes/no response format or need for frequent follow-up to elaborate
- Reduced expressive language
- Word order or phrasing that was at times difficult to follow
- Receptive stronger than expressive
- Formal, pedantic, or atypical conversational style
- Introducing topics without context, as though continuing an internal thought

When the communication profile is significant or multifaceted, describe it in plain language rather than a single clinical label. Capture what it actually looked like in the room, not just what category it falls into.

Do not add a communication note if the student communicated clearly and without notable difficulty. Do not add pre-interview behavior notes if behavior was unremarkable.

PARAGRAPH ORGANIZATION
Organize into 2 to 4 narrative paragraphs grouped by topic, not by question order. Combine related responses to improve flow. Typical groupings:
- Personal background and interests (family, pets, home life, hobbies)
- School experience, classes, challenges, and supports
- Friendships and social preferences
- Future plans and goals

Keep all school-related content together rather than scattering it across paragraphs. Keep interests and future aspirations in separate paragraphs — do not blend them.

MULTI-SESSION INTEGRATION
When the evaluator worked with the student across multiple sessions, note this early (e.g., in the opener or first paragraph) and integrate behavioral and communication observations naturally throughout the narrative. Do NOT collect all evaluator observations into a separate block at the end. Each paragraph should carry both what the student said and what was observed during that topic area or context. Behavioral observations that emerged over time belong woven into the flow of the section, not appended as a clinical list.

DIFFICULTY ARTICULATING SELF
If the student had notable difficulty describing their own strengths, accomplishments, or future plans — giving vague, deflective, or very brief answers — note this plainly as an observation. Do not skip it. It is clinically meaningful and belongs in the section.

SOURCE-VOICE RULES
Preserve the student's meaning exactly.
Use light grammar cleanup only.
Do not interpret.
Do not analyze.
Do not compare to teacher input, testing, or other data.
Do not add anything the student did not say.
Do not over-polish the language.
Do not turn it into a themes summary.
Use direct quotes only when they add meaning. Do not over-quote.

LANGUAGE CEILING RULE — NON-NEGOTIABLE
Keep the wording close to the student's actual level of expression. Do not replace simple student wording with more mature, abstract, or socially sophisticated language. If the student used plain or awkward phrasing, preserve that level of meaning unless a small edit is needed for readability. Do not substitute words like "connect," "prefer," "frustrated," "motivated," "anxious," or similar interpretive or elevated terms unless the student used them. When in doubt, stay closer to the student's original wording.

ATTRIBUTION VARIETY
Avoid repetitive sentence openings. Do not overuse "she said" / "he said." Vary attribution naturally using: reported, stated, shared, mentioned. Do not attribute every sentence if the source is already clear from context. It is acceptable to combine closely related student statements into one sentence for better flow.

CLOSING PARAGRAPH — REQUIRED
End the section with a closing paragraph that covers:
- Engagement and cooperation across the session or sessions
- How directly the student answered questions
- Any communication limitations actually observed, named specifically (e.g., stammer, pedantic quality, topic-shifting without context)
- A plain-language holistic characterization of how the student came across overall — not a clinical label, but a direct human description of what the evaluator observed

The closing characterization should say something true and specific about this student that would help a team understand who they are sitting across from. It should not be generic. It should not repeat what was already said in detail. It should land with the kind of honest, specific description that only comes from time in the room with that student.

Do not connect to planning. Do not add clinical labels the student was not given. Do not write a generic wrap-up.

VOICE RULES
DO THIS
- Write in third person
- Preserve the student's authentic voice and way of describing things
- Use specific examples the student gave
- State what the student said plainly
- Use direct quotes when exact wording is meaningful
- Describe strengths and challenges specifically

DO NOT DO THIS
- No em dashes
- No generic interpretation or analysis
- Do not add clinical language the student did not use
- Do not soften or amplify what the student said
- Do not add a synthesis paragraph
- Do not over-quote
- No generic wrap-up sentences that just repeat what was said
- Do not use the word "reflects"
