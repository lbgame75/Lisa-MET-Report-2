TEACHER INPUT
PURPOSE
Write a clean, natural Teacher Input section based only on teacher questionnaire or teacher-provided data. Preserve authentic teacher meaning and level of concern while organizing the information into readable professional narrative.
NONNEGOTIABLE RULES

Preserve authentic teacher voice
Do not soften concerns
Do not intensify concerns
Do not add concerns not reported
Do not invent skills, supports, needs, or examples
Do not interpret within the individual teacher paragraphs
Do not assume pronouns unless clearly indicated
Use light grammar cleanup only
Do not repeat large blocks of teacher language verbatim
No em dashes
Do not use the word "reflects"
Avoid over-polished transitions and generic wrap-up language
Maintain natural human cadence and a professional, parent-friendly tone

HEADER FORMAT — use exactly:
Mr./Ms. First Last, Subject (submitted MM/DD/YYYY)
FORMAT

Default to one narrative paragraph per teacher
Multiple paragraphs per teacher are appropriate when the teacher provided substantial content or when the material logically divides — but keep all paragraphs for one teacher under that teacher's header as one continuous section
Use direct quotes when the teacher's exact language captures the concern more precisely or plainly than a paraphrase would. When a teacher uses specific, unambiguous wording — especially about a significant concern — preserve it directly rather than smoothing it into a more professional sentence. Paraphrasing away a teacher's plain language often softens the concern. The test: would a paraphrase lose something real? If yes, quote it.
No bullet points within individual teacher narratives

SOURCE VOICE RULE
The individual teacher narrative must stay in source voice.

Preserve the teacher's meaning, priorities, observations, and level of concern
Stay as close to the teacher's actual language as possible, especially when it is plain and specific
Do not smooth a teacher's direct, plain statement into more polished professional language — that smoothing is a loss, not an improvement
Do not add evaluator analysis inside the teacher paragraph
Do not generalize beyond what the teacher reported
Do not insert themes, cross-setting comparisons, eligibility language, or conclusions inside the teacher paragraph
You may reorder information for flow, combine related statements into natural sentences, and correct minor grammar
You may not turn the teacher paragraph into interpretation or add language the teacher did not imply

CLOSING SENTENCE RULE
End each teacher's section with the teacher's own stated conclusion, recommendation, or summary judgment — not an evaluator restatement of it. If the teacher said "she needs structure and fewer distractions," close with that, attributed to the teacher. Do not rephrase it into a cleaner evaluator summary. The teacher's closing thought ends the section; the evaluator's voice does not enter here.

ATTRIBUTION
Use attribution economically. Establish the teacher once at the start, then let the information carry without re-anchoring every sentence. Do not begin multiple consecutive sentences with "He/She reported that."
REDUNDANCY
Do not restate the same concern more than once. If two sentences communicate nearly the same point, combine or delete one.
VOICE GUARDRAILS

Write like a real school psychologist summarizing one teacher form
Prefer direct sentences over polished transitions
Do not use stock transitions such as "in addition," "additionally," "overall," "furthermore," "notably," or similar unless truly necessary
Move naturally and directly from one idea to the next
Keep the writing concrete, school-based, and human

DO NOT INCLUDE

Synthesis paragraph
Themes section
Instructional priorities block
(Those are for clinical reasoning, not the report.)
