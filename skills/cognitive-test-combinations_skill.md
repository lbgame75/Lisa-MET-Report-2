## For WISC-V, WAIS-V, CTONI-2, WJ-IV Cognitive, CTOPP-2, or Combinations

### WHEN TO USE THIS SKILL
You're generating a cognitive section for a MET report. The student has been given one or more standardized cognitive tests. You need to interpret the results, describe patterns of strength and weakness, explain what the scores mean for learning and classroom functioning, and present the data in Lisa's authentic voice.

### WHAT THE USER WILL PROVIDE
1. **Student name and grade**
2. **Test(s) administered** (WISC-V, WAIS-V, CTONI-2, WJ-IV Cognitive, CTOPP-2, or combinations)
3. **Score table(s)** formatted as: Subtest | Scaled/Standard Score | Percentile Rank | Classification
4. **Testing behavior observations** (paragraph, bullets, or transcript)
5. **Framework choice:** "Composite/Index" OR "CHC"
6. **Interpretive Partner mode**: Yes or No

### YOUR JOB — VOICE AND STRUCTURE

#### Testing Behavior Paragraph (Always First)
- How the student engaged: effort level, cooperation, persistence
- Specific observations: "became visibly frustrated," "worked methodically," "asked for clarification"
- Note patterns: "worked quickly on easier items, slowed when tasks grew difficult" (sign of good self-regulation)
- NO validity statement needed; behavior flows naturally into scores
- End with: "She was cooperative (only if this is true) and demonstrated good/adequate/limited effort throughout"

#### Score Table(s)
Present exactly as provided.

#### INTERPRETIVE DEPTH RULE — THIS IS CRITICAL
When a subtest score deviates meaningfully from the overall profile, do not explain it with a textbook description of what the task measures. Instead, analyze the specific process demands of that task and what this student's performance pattern suggests about how their brain approached it. Ask: what does this task actually require the student to do, step by step? What kind of feedback does the task provide? What processing route could produce this score when the rest of the profile looks different? If the first explanation that comes to mind is the generic one (e.g., "this task measures stored knowledge"), go deeper. The generic explanation is almost never wrong, but it is almost never sufficient. A score that breaks from the pattern is telling you something specific about how this student thinks and learns. Find that, not the textbook answer.

#### ADAPTIVE STRUCTURE RULE
When all index scores are not computed (e.g., WAIS-5 FSIQ composite that generates only VCI, FRI, and FSIQ), do not force an index-by-index or subtest-by-subtest structure. Let the data determine the organization. Use ability area references as natural transitions within a flowing narrative ("In terms of her Verbal Comprehension abilities," "In the area of Fluid Reasoning," "Julia's overall Working Memory performance was unique"), not as section headers. Report what matters and is instructionally relevant. When the entire profile falls in the same range, state the general level once, identify what varied and what mattered, and focus interpretation there.

#### Composite/Index Framework (If Selected)

**Structure: Index by Index** (VCI → VSI → FR → WMI → PSI) when all indices are computed.

For each index:
1. **State the score and classification** — "His Verbal Comprehension Index fell in the [range] (VCI [score], [percentile] percentile)"
2. **Describe what he did on the subtests** — specific performance, not generic interpretation
3. **State what it means** — connect to the underlying ability
4. **Classroom connection** — one to two sentences, specific to THIS student

**Example (Verbal Comprehension Index):**
His Verbal Comprehension Index fell in the Average range (VCI 108, 70th percentile). On Similarities, he identified relationships between word pairs accurately and could explain the categories with clear language. On Vocabulary, he defined most common words precisely, though he struggled with less familiar words like "perspicacious" and "auspicious." His verbal reasoning and language development are age-appropriate. In the classroom, he can follow grade-level verbal instruction and participate in class discussions, though complex or abstract language may require clarification.

**Tightening rules:**
- Do NOT spend sentences explaining what each ability is (this is not an educational document)
- Move quickly to what the student did and what it means
- ONE to TWO sentences of classroom connection per index, specific to the student
- No generic implications

#### CHC Framework (If Selected)

**Structure: Ability by Ability** (Gf, Gc, Gsm, Glr, Gv, Ga, Gs, with focus on those relevant to school psychology)

For each broad ability tested:
1. **State the performance level** — "His Fluid Reasoning (Gf) performance fell in the [range]"
2. **Describe the narrow abilities that make up this broad ability** — what subtests/tasks measure Gf? What did he do on those?
3. **State the pattern** — what does his performance tell us about this ability?
4. **Classroom meaning** — how does this ability (or weakness) show up in classroom learning?

**Example (Fluid Reasoning):**
His Fluid Reasoning (Gf) fell in the Low Average range. Fluid reasoning is the ability to work through problems that are new — where a student cannot rely on what is already memorized or practiced. On Matrix Reasoning, he identified patterns in simpler visual items but lost accuracy as the patterns grew more complex. On Figure Weights, he made errors even on easier items that required him to work out the logical relationship between groups of objects. In math and other courses where new concepts build on each other, he is likely to need the steps shown to him explicitly rather than being expected to figure out the approach on his own.

**Tightening rules (same as Composite/Index):**
- Less explaining what the ability is
- Faster move to what the student did and what it means
- One to two sentences of classroom meaning per ability, specific to the student
- No generic implications

#### Handling Multi-Test Combinations

**WISC + WJ (Cognitive):**
- WISC provides composite/index structure
- WJ provides CHC-organized subtests
- Integrate by ability rather than test: "His reasoning ability (measured by WISC Matrix Reasoning and WJ Concept Formation) fell in the [range], showing..."
- Do NOT organize by test name; organize by the cognitive abilities being assessed

**WISC + CTOPP + WJ:**
- Follow same integration rule
- Group related subtests: auditory processing (CTOPP tasks) alongside related WISC subtests
- Example: "Phonological Awareness (CTOPP Phoneme Deletion) and related auditory processing tasks were significantly weak, indicating..."

**CTONI-2 (used for non-verbal, language-different, or specific reasoning assessment):**

Follow the four-paragraph structure after the score table:
1. Full Scale and pictorial/geometric comparison
2. Plain-language explanation of Analogies, Categories, Sequences task types — describe what each task actually requires the student to do (e.g., Categories: "she looked at a set of pictures and selected which one from five options belonged with the group"), not a generic definition
3. Student's specific subtest pattern by task type in parent-friendly language, with process-level interpretation of why specific tasks were harder or easier given what they demand
4. So What connecting to classroom and transition, naming generalization limits when profile supports it

Model: Julia Jose CTONI-2 section (March 2026) and Wyatt Bushway report (April 2026).

#### Score Classifications (Standard Application)
- **130+**: Very High / Superior
- **120-129**: High / High Average
- **110-119**: Average / High Average (for indices/subtests, this is often called "High Average")
- **90-109**: Average
- **80-89**: Low Average / Below Average (for indices)
- **70-79**: Low / Well Below Average
- **Below 70**: Very Low / Significantly Below Average

#### Special Patterns to Address (If Present)

**Significant Index Spread (WISC example: VCI 118, PSI 82):**
- Do NOT use "contrast" language ("it's not verbal, it's processing speed")
- Instead: "His verbal reasoning is a clear strength, while his processing speed is notably weaker"
- Explain what this means: "This discrepancy affects his ability to [specific classroom task]"

**Working Memory vs. Processing Speed Difference:**
- If WMI > PSI: "His ability to hold and manipulate information is stronger than his ability to process information quickly. In classroom settings, this means he benefits from time to think and organize his response, but may struggle with timed tasks."
- If PSI > WMI: "His processing speed is a relative strength, but his working memory is weaker. This means he works at a good pace but may lose track of instructions or information he's trying to hold in mind during multi-step tasks."

**VAL (Verbal Analogies Learning) vs. Story Recall (WJ tests):**
- If VAL > Story Recall: "His ability to learn associations is stronger than his ability to learn meaning from context, suggesting strength with rote or paired learning and possible difficulty with narrative or contextual memory"
- If Story Recall > VAL: "His ability to learn from meaningful context is stronger than rote association, suggesting he learns better when information is woven into a story or connected to prior knowledge"

**Unexpected subtest deviations:**
- Apply the Interpretive Depth Rule. Do not default to textbook explanations.
- Analyze the specific process demands of the task: what kind of feedback does it provide? What processing route could produce this score? What does the student's approach tell you about how their brain is working?
- Example: Digits Backward at 6 when Forward and Sequencing are both at 3 — consider whether the backward task engaged a visual-spatial strategy rather than an auditory one, especially if visual-spatial processing is a relative strength across evaluations.

#### Overall Summary Paragraph (Final Paragraph)
- One paragraph that brings the pattern together
- Do NOT repeat individual scores or index interpretations
- Focus on: What is the overall cognitive picture? What are the concrete implications for this student's learning?
- Example: "Marcus's cognitive profile shows average verbal reasoning and comprehension alongside significant weakness in working memory and processing speed. He can follow and understand material when it is explained clearly, but holding multiple pieces of information while actively working is hard for him, and he works more slowly than most of his peers. In the classroom, he benefits from written directions he can refer back to, tasks broken into steps, and adequate time to finish."
- NOTE: When not all indices are computed or when the profile is uniformly low, this summary paragraph may not be needed. If the narrative interpretation already tells the complete story, just stop.

### LISA'S VOICE RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

#### DO THIS
- Start with observable performance (what the student did) before moving to interpretation
- Use specific subtest names: "On Digit Span forward, he recalled..." (not "on memory tasks")
- Describe what he could do AND what was hard: "He solved the first three problems accurately but struggled with the pattern in more complex items"
- State classroom meaning specifically: "When directions are given verbally without writing, he will struggle to follow them" (not "verbal comprehension affects learning")
- Use direct language: "He could not..." "He struggled with..." "This makes it difficult..."

#### DO NOT DO THIS
- No em dashes (ever)
- No negative parallelisms: "it's not reasoning, it's memory" — FORBIDDEN in any form
- No short punchy pairs: "He can reason. That's not the problem."
- No performative setups: "What's worth examining is..."
- No generic wrap-ups that repeat what was just said
- No overpathologizing Low Average (11th-24th percentile) scores — state them plainly
- No "reflects," "indicates," "demonstrates" overuse (max 1-2 per section)
- No abstract claims: "processing speed affects learning" — instead: "his slow processing speed makes it hard for him to complete timed math facts"

### INTERPRETIVE PARTNER MODE (IF USER SELECTED YES)

After the full cognitive section, shift to Interpretive Mode and address:

Pattern observations:
- What's the most striking part of this profile (biggest strength, most significant weakness)?
- Are there index/ability discrepancies that stand out?
- What underlying cognitive processing issues do you see?
- How well do the cognitive scores align with the academic profile (if you have achievement data)?
- For any score that deviates from the overall pattern, offer a process-level hypothesis about why

Classroom implications:
- What specific classroom tasks will be hardest for this student?
- What cognitive support will he need to access grade-level content?
- Are there specific subjects where his cognitive profile will create the biggest barrier?

SLD / other eligibility thinking:
- Does this profile support a specific LD pattern?
- If PSW (Pattern of Strengths and Weaknesses), where's the processing deficit and where's the achievement impact?
- What other data would help clarify the picture?

What I'm noticing:
- [1-3 key observations about the cognitive profile]
- Questions to bring to the team
- Considerations for instructional approach

Keep this brief and directly tied to the data provided.

### RED FLAGS FOR VOICE DRIFT

- Does this sound like a direct evaluation or like smoothed AI output?
- Did I describe what the student actually did on subtests?
- Did I avoid any negative parallelisms or contrast constructions?
- Did I make classroom connections specific to THIS student?
- Am I repeating information from section to section?
- Did I classify scores accurately without overpathologizing Low Average?
- Is every sentence about this specific student, not generic?
- Did I apply the Interpretive Depth Rule to every score that deviates from the overall pattern, or did I default to textbook explanations?
