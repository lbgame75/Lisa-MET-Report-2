### FOR EACH DOMAIN — USE THIS EXACT STRUCTURE

**Domain Name and Definition (First Sentence):**
State what the domain measures with specific functional description.

Example: "The Physical scale measures large muscle coordination and fine motor control used for movement and classroom tasks."

**Developmental Levels (Second Sentence):**
State parent and teacher age equivalents and general developmental range.

Example: "Parent ratings place Suma in the early preschool range, while teacher ratings place him in the late toddler range. Across settings, motor functioning falls between approximately 2 and 4 years of age and is significantly below fifth-grade expectations."

Or with aligned ratings: "Both parent and teacher ratings place Suma's adaptive functioning in the toddler range."

**Abilities Paragraph (What Student CAN Do):**
- Describe specific skills documented as YES in item analysis from both raters
- Use concrete, observable examples
- If home and school are consistent, state that
- If there's a meaningful difference, describe it clearly
- End with statement of what these skills show: "These skills show..."

Example: "Across home and school, Suma walks independently, climbs stairs with support, carries objects safely, and demonstrates basic hand control such as twisting motions and stacking objects. These skills show stable gross motor mobility in both environments."

Or with difference: "At home, he demonstrates [skills]. At school, he shows [skills]. These abilities indicate..."

**Unmet Skills Paragraph (What Student CANNOT Yet Do):**
- Describe specific skills documented as NO in item analysis
- State skills that are not emerging across settings
- If there's a meaningful home-school difference, name it
- Describe what this pattern means for functioning

Example: "However, both raters report that he does not consistently catch a ball, balance on one foot, jump over obstacles, cut with scissors independently, draw shapes such as a square, or tie shoelaces. Fine motor precision and coordinated motor planning remain significantly delayed."

Or with difference: "At school, he does not complete assignments independently or manage materials. At home, he does not independently manage safety information or personal care needs."

**Educational Impact Sentence (Final Sentence of Domain):**
One clear sentence connecting to classroom or school functioning.

Example: "Motor abilities support independent movement but limit participation in structured fine motor tasks required in the classroom."

Or: "Adaptive functioning indicates consistent dependence on direct adult prompting and supervision across settings."

## INTEGRATION RULE — PARENT AND TEACHER

**Within each domain paragraph:**
- Combine both raters naturally
- Use "Across settings..." or "In both environments..." when consistent
- Use "At home..." / "At school..." when there's a meaningful difference
- Do NOT separate into parent-only and teacher-only paragraphs
- DO state which scores come from which rater when describing age equivalents

**Example of good integration:**
"Parent ratings place Suma in the early preschool range, while teacher ratings place him in the late toddler range. Across settings, motor functioning falls between approximately 2 and 4 years of age. Across home and school, Suma walks independently and climbs stairs with support. At school, he demonstrates stronger ability with scissors than at home, though neither shows independent cutting skill."

**Example of what NOT to do:**
- Separate "Parent Perspective" and "Teacher Perspective" sections
- Write only from parent data in first paragraph, teacher data in second
- List items without connecting them to observable functioning

---

## CRITICAL ACCURACY RULES

**Item-Level Accuracy — NON-NEGOTIABLE:**
- Every ability listed must be documented as YES in the item analysis of the uploaded DP-4 report. Every unmet skill must be documented as NO in the item analysis of the uploaded DP-4 report.
- Do NOT use generic developmental descriptions from memory or training data. If the report shows "catches a ball" as NO and "walks independently" as YES, those are the items you report — not generic category language like "gross motor skills are emerging."
- When the skill is invoked, the evaluator will upload the computer-generated DP-4 report with Yes/No item-level data. Read that report to identify: the highest Yes items (what the student CAN do — the ceiling of functioning in each domain) and the lowest No items (where functioning breaks down — the floor of unmet skills). Report a representative sample from both ends, not every item.
- Use specific item text from the report translated into natural readable language. Do not substitute generic milestone descriptions for the actual documented items.
- Do not invent skills or capabilities.

**Age Equivalent Accuracy:**
- State exactly what both reports provide
- Make clear comparison to student's current age
- Use phrase "significantly below [age] expectations"

**Home-School Consistency:**
- If both raters mark YES for a skill, use "across settings" language
- If both mark NO, use "both raters report" or "across settings"
- Only note differences when they are meaningful and documented in both reports
- Do not speculate about why differences exist

**Final Data Check:**
- Verify every ability statement against Yes items
- Verify every unmet skill statement against No items
- Ensure age equivalents are exact from the reports
- Check that educational impact sentences are concrete, not vague

## EXAMPLE DOMAIN PARAGRAPHS (From Gold Standard)

**Physical:** "The Physical scale measures large muscle coordination and fine motor control used for movement and classroom tasks. Parent ratings place Suma in the early preschool range, while teacher ratings place him in the late toddler range. Across settings, motor functioning falls between approximately 2 and 4 years of age and is significantly below fifth-grade expectations. Across home and school, Suma walks independently, climbs stairs with support, carries objects safely, and demonstrates basic hand control such as twisting motions and stacking objects. These skills show stable gross motor mobility in both environments. However, both raters report that he does not consistently catch a ball, balance on one foot, jump over obstacles, cut with scissors independently, draw shapes such as a square, or tie shoelaces. Fine motor precision and coordinated motor planning remain significantly delayed. Motor abilities support independent movement but limit participation in structured fine motor tasks required in the classroom."

**Adaptive Behavior:** "The Adaptive Behavior scale measures independence in daily routines, self-care, safety, and task completion. Both parent and teacher ratings place Suma's adaptive functioning in the toddler range. In both environments, Suma can drink from a cup, use utensils, remove some clothing items, assist with dressing, and use basic digital devices. He demonstrates awareness of hunger or thirst and can follow simple routines when prompted. These skills show participation in daily routines with support. However, across settings he does not dress independently, does not manage toileting independently for bowel movements, does not clean up after activities, does not use coping strategies when upset, and does not complete tasks without frequent adult reminders. At school, he does not complete assignments independently or manage materials. At home, he does not independently manage safety information or personal care needs. Adaptive functioning indicates consistent dependence on direct adult prompting and supervision across settings."

**Social-Emotional:** "The Social-Emotional scale measures interaction, engagement, and awareness of social expectations. Parent ratings place Suma's social functioning in the infant range, while teacher ratings place him in the very early toddler range. Across both settings, social development remains significantly below age expectations. Across settings, Suma responds to familiar adults, shows enjoyment during preferred activities, reacts differently to familiar versus unfamiliar people, and can be calmed through adult contact. These behaviors demonstrate basic emotional responsiveness and attachment. However, both raters indicate that he does not initiate peer interaction, does not sustain play with other children, does not engage independently in structured tasks for extended periods, does not demonstrate awareness of others' feelings, and does not adjust behavior based on social setting. He needs direct adult support to interact with peers and participate in group activities."
