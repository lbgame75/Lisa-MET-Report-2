LISA-SPECIFIC VOICE RULES TO CHECK
HARD STOPS — If present, section needs rewrite

Em dashes — ANY em dashes = rewrite
Negative parallelisms in ANY form:

"It's not X, it's Y" format
"This is not a [thing], this is a [thing]"
Short punchy pairs: "She can spell. That is not the limitation."


Performative setup sentences:

"What's worth examining is..."
"It's important to note that..."



MODERATE FLAGS — Usually signal AI drift

Excessive "reflects," "indicates," "demonstrates," "appears" — More than 1-2 per section = rewrite
Copula avoidance — using "serves as," "functions as" instead of "is/are"
Generic wrap-up sentences — repeating what was just said
Vague attributions — "research suggests," "studies show," "experts indicate" (when no source cited)
Abstract claims not tied to specific student data

MILD FLAGS — Often AI patterns

Significance inflation — words like "pivotal," "vital," "crucial"
Superficial -ing phrases — tacked on for seeming depth
Promotional or softening language
Hyphenated pairs used with mechanical consistency
Rule of three — three parallel items listed for seeming profundity

QUALITY CHECKS (Writing Craft)

Specificity: Does every claim tie to actual student data?
Concrete examples: Do error patterns show actual examples?
Paragraph flow: Does each paragraph have one job and stop when done?
Classroom connection: Is it specific to THIS student in THIS classroom?
Severity match: Do serious behavioral concerns sound serious?

RED FLAGS THAT MEAN STOP AND REWRITE
If your draft contains ANY of these:

Em dashes
"It's not X, it's Y" construction
"What is worth examining" or "It's important to note"
More than 2 instances of "reflects" in one section
A paragraph with no specific student data
Behavioral concerns that sound understated or softened
A final paragraph that just repeats what you already said
