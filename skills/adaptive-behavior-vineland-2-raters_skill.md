## HARD PROHIBITIONS — NON-NEGOTIABLE

These apply to every sentence in this section. Violating any of them means the output does not sound like Lisa and must be rewritten.

- **No em dashes.** Ever. Use commas or periods instead.
- **No v-scale scores in the narrative.** Ever. Domain standard scores and percentiles only.
- **No age equivalents** unless the evaluator explicitly requests them. If the student is older or the evaluator has overridden this, omit entirely.
- **No jargon lists.** Do not write sentences like "compound sentences, possessives, giving directions, and describing experiences in detail are not established." That sentence means nothing to a parent. Say what the student cannot do and why it matters.
- **No X/Y contrast constructions** in any form. No "it is not X, it is Y." No punchy contrast pairs. No sentences that define something by what it is not.
- **No performative setup sentences.** Do not write "What is worth noting here is..." or any version of that construction. Just say the thing.
- **No generic wrap-up sentences** that summarize what was just said.
- **Do not use the word "reflects."**
- **Do not separate parent and teacher into separate paragraphs within a domain.** Integrate both using "at home" and "at school" as the organizing frame.
- **No score dumping.** Do not list every subdomain score. State domain scores, then move immediately into what the student actually does.
- **No closure paragraph.** The section ends where the data end. No synthesis, no eligibility language, no recommendations.

## PLAIN-LANGUAGE TEST — REQUIRED BEFORE FINALIZING ANY SENTENCE

Before writing any sentence about what the student can or cannot do, ask: would a parent reading this know exactly what this student can and cannot actually do?

If the sentence requires clinical background to interpret, it fails the test. Rewrite it in plain language and support it with one concrete example from the item analysis.

Examples of sentences that fail the test:
- "Expressive grammar is incomplete." (What does that mean for this student?)
- "Receptive skills are limited in the area of inferential processing." (What cannot he do?)
- "Adaptive functioning is consistent with cognitive findings." (This belongs in Summary, not here.)

Examples that pass:
- "He cannot retell a familiar story, describe what happened in his day, or give someone directions."
- "When asked why he is upset, he says 'I'm sad' but cannot explain further."
- "He cannot make a phone call, find a needed number, or notify anyone of his plans when going out."

## FOR EACH DOMAIN — STRUCTURE AND RULES

**Opening sentence:** State the domain name, both standard scores, and both percentiles in one sentence. Example: "The parent Communication domain score was 73 (4th percentile) and the teacher Communication domain score was 41 (less than 1st percentile)."

Do not state subdomain scores in the narrative. Do not state age equivalents unless evaluator has explicitly requested them.

**Narrative paragraphs:** Write 2 to 4 paragraphs per domain. Organize by functional theme, not by subdomain label. Use "at home" and "at school" as the frame for integrating both raters.

The narrative job for each domain is:
1. What can this student do, and how far does that get him in daily life?
2. Where does functioning break down, and what does that look like in concrete terms?
3. Where is the home-school gap meaningful, and what does it tell the next team?

For each functional pattern, name the pattern first, then support it with one or two specific examples from the item analysis. Do not list every item. Choose the examples that best illustrate the pattern.

**End:** Stop when the data for that domain are complete. No summary sentence. No wrap-up. No bridge to the next domain.

---

## ITEM SOURCING — NON-NEGOTIABLE

All item-level content must come directly from the uploaded Vineland-3 Comprehensive Reports. Do not use generic milestone language from memory or training data.

How to use the item analysis:
- What the student CAN do: Look at the highest-numbered items scored 2 (usually performed independently) within each subdomain. These are the ceiling of functioning. Pick the most meaningful examples, not every item.
- What the student CANNOT yet do: Look at the lowest-numbered items scored 0 or 1 (never or sometimes performed independently). These are the first unmastered skills. Pick examples that illustrate the functional gap clearly.
- Home-school differences: If the same item is scored differently across raters, that difference belongs in the narrative when it is meaningful.

---

## TRANSITION-FOCUSED REPORTING

For students who are 16 or older, or who are in self-contained programs at any age, the narrative must be written with an eye toward what the next program needs to know. This means:

- Community navigation, money management, employment readiness, and independent travel are high-priority areas. State what is and is not established, concretely.
- Safety awareness gaps (recognizing when someone may intend harm, avoiding being taken advantage of) are meaningful and should be named directly.
- Domestic and self-care skills should be described in terms of what level of support the student will need, not just what he can or cannot do.
- Do not editorialize or make recommendations. State the functional picture and let the team draw the conclusions.
